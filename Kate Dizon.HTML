Food preservation in aquaculture — keeping harvests safe and valuable

Practical techniques for handling, storing, and processing fish, shrimp, mollusks and seaweed to reduce spoilage, maintain quality, and increase shelf life.

Who this guide is for

Small-scale fish farmers, hatcheries, processors and extension workers.

Why it matters

Preservation reduces post-harvest losses, improves food safety, and increases income.

Quick checklist
  • Fast chilling after harvest
  • Clean handling and hygienic surfaces
  • Appropriate salt/smoke/drying methods where cold chain isn't available
  • Use of simple packaging and labeling
Explore methods

Common preservation methods

Cooling & Cold Chain

Rapid chilling (icing, refrigerated seawater, ice slurry) immediately after harvest slows bacterial growth and enzymatic decay.

  • Ice: crushed ice in insulated containers
  • Refrigeration: 0–4°C for most fish
  • Freezing: -18°C or colder for long-term storage

Drying & Salting

Traditional and low-cost method – remove moisture so microbes cannot grow.

  • Sun-drying on racks (protect from flies)
  • Solar dryers for better control
  • Salt-curing: dry-salting or brine salting

Smoking

Heat and smoke impart flavor and reduce moisture; use proper smoking temps to ensure safety.

  • Hot smoking cooks and preserves
  • Cold smoking adds flavor but needs other barriers

Canning & Thermal Processing

Commercially sealed and heat-treated products have long shelf life if processed correctly.

  • Pressure canning for low-acid foods (fish, shrimp)
  • Follow validated time/temperature schedules

Fermentation

Used for value-added products (fish sauce, fermented shrimp). Requires hygienic process control.

  • Controlled salt percentage and containers

Modified Atmosphere & Vacuum Packaging

Pack fish in low-oxygen or vacuum to slow oxidation and microbial growth — often combined with refrigeration.

Harvest handling & hygiene

Good preservation starts on the farm: reduce stress and contamination at harvest.

  • Use clean nets and containers; avoid prolonged exposure to heat.
  • Bleed and gut promptly if required for quality.
  • Keep surfaces and equipment sanitized; train staff in hand hygiene.

Food safety risks & how to control them

Common hazards
  • Bacterial growth (e.g., Vibrio, Salmonella)
  • Histamine formation in tuna, mackerel
  • Parasitic risks in raw products
Control measures
  • Time–temperature control: keep below safe temperatures
  • Sanitation and traceability
  • Use validated processing procedures

Simple case: extending shelf life of tilapia for local markets

  1. Harvest in the morning when water temperatures are cooler.
  2. Immediately place fish on crushed ice in insulated boxes (1:1 fish:ice by volume).
  3. At market, keep fish on ice and covered; sell within 24–48 hours.
  4. If refrigeration is unavailable, consider solar-drying fillets or salt-curing.

Tools & resources

Low-cost equipment
  • Insulated fish boxes and crushed ice
  • Simple solar dryer or raised drying racks
  • Smokehouse or cabinet smoker
Record-keeping

Label batches with harvest date, method used and destination — helps trace problems and manage stocks.
